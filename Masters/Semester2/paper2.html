Chapter 01

Meaning, Characteristics, Objectives and Scope of Educational Research

1.1 Meaning of Educational Research

Educational research is a systematic, scientific and objective investigation conducted to discover facts, verify knowledge, and solve problems related to education. It studies teaching methods, learning processes, curriculum, evaluation, educational technology, administration, and psychological aspects of learners.

In Simple Words

Educational research is the scientific study of educational problems to improve teaching–learning and educational practices.

1.2 Characteristics of Educational Research

  1. SystematicIt follows a planned and organized procedure.
  2. ScientificIt uses scientific methods like observation, experimentation, surveys, and statistical analysis.
  3. ObjectiveIt avoids personal bias and is based on facts and evidence.
  4. LogicalConclusions are drawn through reasoning and analysis.
  5. EmpiricalIt is based on real data and observation.
  6. ReplicableThe study can be repeated by others to verify results.
  7. Problem-OrientedIt focuses on solving educational problems.
  8. PurposefulIt aims at improvement of educational practices.

1.3 Objectives of Educational Research

  1. To discover new facts and principles in education.
  2. To verify and test existing educational theories.
  3. To identify problems in teaching–learning processes.
  4. To improve curriculum, methods of teaching, and evaluation systems.
  5. To study learner behavior and psychological factors.
  6. To provide solutions for educational administration and planning.
  7. To improve the quality of education.
  8. To develop innovative instructional strategies and technologies.

1.4 Scope of Educational Research

The scope of educational research is very wide and includes the following areas:

A. Teaching–Learning Process

  • Methods of teaching
  • Learning strategies
  • Classroom management

B. Curriculum Development

  • Designing curriculum
  • Evaluating curriculum
  • Updating syllabus

C. Educational Psychology

  • Intelligence
  • Motivation
  • Personality
  • Mental health

D. Educational Technology

  • Use of ICT
  • Online learning
  • Smart classrooms
  • Instructional design

E. Evaluation and Assessment

  • Examination system
  • Measurement techniques
  • Test construction

F. Educational Administration

  • School management
  • Leadership
  • Policy implementation

G. Inclusive and Special Education

  • Special needs education
  • Guidance and counselling

Educational research plays a vital role in improving the quality, effectiveness, and efficiency of education. It provides a scientific basis for solving educational problems and developing better teaching–learning practices.

Chapter 02

Types of Educational Research: Fundamental, Applied and Action Research

Educational research is broadly classified into three major types: Fundamental (Basic) Research, Applied Research, and Action Research.

2.1 Fundamental (Basic) Research

Meaning

Fundamental research is conducted to develop new theories, principles, or knowledge in education. It is concerned with advancing knowledge, not immediate practical use.

Characteristics

  • Theory-oriented
  • Conducted in controlled conditions
  • Aims at generalization
  • Expands existing knowledge
  • Not immediately concerned with solving classroom problems

Purpose

  • To test existing theories
  • To develop new educational principles
  • To explain educational phenomena
Examples
Study of the relationship between intelligence and academic achievement.
Research on cognitive development theories.

2.2 Applied Research

Meaning

Applied research is conducted to solve specific practical problems in education using scientific methods. It applies the findings of fundamental research to real-life situations.

Characteristics

  • Problem-oriented
  • Practical in nature
  • Conducted in real educational settings
  • Seeks immediate application
  • Limited generalization

Purpose

  • To improve teaching methods
  • To develop effective curriculum
  • To improve evaluation techniques
Examples
Studying the effectiveness of mobile learning in higher secondary schools.
Comparing two teaching methods to improve mathematics achievement.

2.3 Action Research

Meaning

Action research is a small-scale research conducted by teachers to solve immediate classroom problems. It is focused on improving one's own teaching practices.

Characteristics

  • Conducted by teachers
  • Small scale
  • Immediate solution-oriented
  • Cyclical process (Plan → Act → Observe → Reflect)
  • Local in nature

Purpose

  • To improve classroom teaching
  • To solve specific student problems
  • To enhance teaching effectiveness
Examples
A teacher testing a new method to reduce student absenteeism.
Trying a new strategy to improve reading skills.

2.4 Comparison of the Three Types

BasisFundamentalAppliedAction
AimDevelop theorySolve practical problemsSolve classroom problems
NatureTheoreticalPracticalImmediate & local
ScaleLarge scaleMedium/LargeSmall scale
Conducted byResearchersResearchers/InstitutionsTeachers
GeneralizationHighLimitedVery limited

Fundamental research builds knowledge, applied research uses that knowledge to solve practical problems, and action research improves classroom practices directly.

Chapter 03

Methods of Educational Research

Educational research uses different methods to study problems scientifically. The important methods are: Survey Method, Experimental Method, Descriptive Method, and Historical Method.

3.1 Survey Method

Meaning

The survey method is used to collect data from a large group of people to study their opinions, attitudes, behaviors, or characteristics.

Purpose

  • To gather information about present conditions
  • To study trends and patterns
  • To analyze attitudes and opinions

Tools Used

  • Questionnaires
  • Interviews
  • Rating scales
  • Checklists

Characteristics

  • Large sample size
  • Data collected at one point of time
  • Mostly quantitative
  • Cost-effective
Example
Survey on students' attitude toward online learning. Survey on use of educational technology in schools.

3.2 Experimental Method

Meaning

The experimental method studies the effect of one variable on another under controlled conditions. It establishes a cause-and-effect relationship.

Key Elements

  • Independent variable (manipulated)
  • Dependent variable (measured)
  • Control group and experimental group

Characteristics

  • Controlled conditions
  • Scientific and systematic
  • Objective measurement
  • Valid and reliable results

Purpose

  • To test hypotheses
  • To determine effectiveness of teaching methods
Example
Comparing traditional teaching with smart classroom teaching to measure achievement.

3.3 Descriptive Method

Meaning

The descriptive method describes and interprets the existing conditions, practices, and relationships. It focuses on "what exists" rather than cause and effect.

Characteristics

  • Non-experimental
  • Studies present conditions
  • Uses surveys, observations, case studies

Purpose

  • To describe behavior, trends, or characteristics
  • To find relationships between variables

Types

  • Survey studies
  • Case studies
  • Correlational studies
  • Developmental studies
Example
Study of learning styles of higher secondary students. Study of classroom management practices.

3.4 Historical Method

Meaning

The historical method studies past events, developments, and experiences in education to understand present conditions and predict future trends.

Sources of Data

  • Primary sources (letters, government records, diaries)
  • Secondary sources (books, journals, reports)

Characteristics

  • Systematic collection of past records
  • Critical evaluation of documents
  • Interpretation of historical facts

Purpose

  • To understand educational development
  • To trace evolution of educational policies
Example
Study of the development of education policy in India. Analysis of past curriculum reforms.

3.5 Comparison of Methods

BasisSurveyExperimentalDescriptiveHistorical
FocusPresent opinionsCause-effectExisting conditionsPast events
ControlNo controlHigh controlNo manipulationNo manipulation
TimePresentPresent/FuturePresentPast
NatureData collectionScientific testingDescribing factsDocument analysis

Survey collects broad information, Experimental establishes cause-effect, Descriptive explains current conditions, and Historical studies past developments. Together, they strengthen educational research and improve educational practices.

Chapter 04

Qualitative and Quantitative Research in Education

4.1 Qualitative Research in Education

Meaning

Qualitative research focuses on understanding human behavior, experiences, meanings, and social contexts in education. It answers questions like "why" and "how."

Characteristics

  • Deals with non-numerical data (words, descriptions, observations)
  • Small sample size
  • In-depth and detailed study
  • Flexible research design
  • Subjective interpretation

Methods Used

  • Interviews
  • Observations
  • Case studies
  • Focus group discussions
  • Document analysis

Purpose

  • To understand students' experiences
  • To explore attitudes, beliefs, and perceptions
  • To study classroom interactions
Example
A case study on the emotional adjustment of adolescents.
Interview-based study on teachers' perception of inclusive education.

4.2 Quantitative Research in Education

Meaning

Quantitative research focuses on collecting numerical data and analyzing it statistically to test hypotheses and measure relationships. It answers questions like "how much" or "to what extent."

Characteristics

  • Uses numerical data
  • Large sample size
  • Structured tools
  • Statistical analysis
  • Objective and measurable

Methods Used

  • Surveys with structured questionnaires
  • Experiments
  • Standardized tests
  • Correlational studies

Purpose

  • To measure academic achievement
  • To test effectiveness of teaching methods
  • To examine relationships between variables
Example
Experimental study comparing two teaching methods.
Survey measuring students' achievement scores.

4.3 Comparison and Similarities

BasisQualitativeQuantitative
Nature of DataWords, descriptionsNumbers, statistics
Sample SizeSmallLarge
PurposeUnderstandingMeasurement & testing
ApproachSubjectiveObjective
AnalysisThematicStatistical
Research DesignFlexibleStructured

Similarities

  • Both are systematic and scientific.
  • Both aim to solve educational problems.
  • Both follow research steps (problem, data collection, analysis, conclusion).

Qualitative research provides deep understanding of educational situations, while quantitative research provides measurable and generalizable results. Both approaches are complementary and together strengthen educational research.

Chapter 05

Formulation of Research Problem

Formulation of a research problem is the first and most important step in educational research. A research problem is a clear, specific and researchable statement about an issue that the researcher wants to investigate. Without a properly defined problem, research cannot proceed systematically.

5.1 Meaning of Research Problem

A research problem is a statement that identifies: what is to be studied, why it is to be studied, and for whom it is relevant. It should clearly state the variables and the population involved.

Example
"Effect of blended learning on academic achievement of higher secondary students."

5.2 Criteria for Selecting a Good Research Problem

  1. ClarityClearly stated and specific; free from ambiguity.
  2. ResearchabilityCan be studied scientifically; data can be collected and measured.
  3. FeasibilityWithin time, cost, and resource limits; suitable to researcher's ability.
  4. SignificanceContributes to knowledge; useful for educational improvement.
  5. Ethical AcceptabilityDoes not harm participants; follows ethical standards.
  6. Availability of DataRequired data and tools should be accessible.

5.3 Sources for Identifying Research Problems

  1. Classroom ExperienceProblems faced in teaching-learning; student behavior issues.
  2. Review of Related LiteratureGaps in previous research; recommendations of earlier studies.
  3. Educational Policies and ReportsGovernment education policies; curriculum reforms.
  4. Personal Interest and ExperienceResearcher's specialization; practical difficulties observed.
  5. Social IssuesInclusive education; mental health of students; technology integration.
  6. Discussion with ExpertsGuidance from supervisors; academic seminars.

5.4 Steps in Formulating a Research Problem

  1. Identify a broad area of interest.
  2. Review related literature.
  3. Identify gaps or issues.
  4. Narrow down to a specific problem.
  5. Define variables clearly.
  6. State the problem in clear terms.

5.5 Defining the Research Problem

Defining the problem means explaining it clearly and precisely. It includes the following components:

  1. Conceptual DefinitionExplaining key concepts in general terms. Example: "Blended learning refers to the combination of online and face-to-face instruction."
  2. Operational DefinitionDefining how variables will be measured. Example: "Academic achievement refers to marks obtained in the final examination."
  3. DelimitationSpecifying the population, area, and time period.
  4. Statement of the ProblemIt should be written in clear sentence form. Example: "A study of the effectiveness of blended learning on academic achievement of higher secondary students of Bongaigaon district."

Characteristics of a Well-Defined Research Problem

  • Specific
  • Measurable
  • Limited in scope
  • Clearly identifies variables
  • Indicates population

The formulation and proper definition of a research problem is the foundation of successful educational research. A clearly defined problem guides the researcher in selecting methodology, tools, and analysis procedures.

Chapter 06

Research Question and Objectives

After defining the research problem, the researcher formulates research questions and objectives to guide the study.

6.1 Research Question

Meaning

A research question is a clear, focused, and specific question that the researcher intends to answer through the study. It guides data collection, research design, and analysis and interpretation.

Characteristics of a Good Research Question

  1. Clear and specific
  2. Researchable (data can be collected)
  3. Focused (not too broad)
  4. Relevant to the problem
  5. Ethically acceptable

Types of Research Questions

  1. Descriptive Research QuestionSeeks to describe a condition or situation. Example: What is the level of academic stress among higher secondary students?
  2. Comparative Research QuestionCompares two or more groups. Example: Is there a difference in achievement between boys and girls?
  3. Relationship (Correlational) Research QuestionExamines relationship between variables. Example: Is there a relationship between study habits and academic achievement?
  4. Causal Research QuestionStudies cause-effect relationship. Example: Does blended learning improve students' academic achievement?

6.2 Research Objectives

Meaning

Research objectives are clear statements describing what the researcher intends to achieve in the study. They are derived from the research problem and questions. Objectives state the specific goals of the research.

Characteristics of Good Research Objectives

  • Clearly stated
  • Specific and measurable
  • Achievable
  • Relevant to the problem
  • Time-bound (if applicable)

Types of Objectives

  1. General ObjectiveBroad aim of the study. Example: To study the effectiveness of blended learning on academic achievement.
  2. Specific ObjectivesDetailed and precise goals. Example: To compare academic achievement of students taught through traditional and blended methods; To find out the relationship between ICT usage and academic performance.

6.3 Comparison: Research Question vs. Objectives

BasisResearch QuestionResearch Objectives
FormQuestion formStatement form
PurposeWhat to find outWhat to achieve
NatureInquiry-basedGoal-oriented
ExampleDoes ICT improve achievement?To examine the effect of ICT on achievement

Importance

  • Provide direction to research
  • Help in selecting research design
  • Guide tool construction
  • Ensure focus and clarity

Research questions define what the study seeks to answer, while objectives state what the researcher aims to achieve. Together, they provide a clear roadmap for conducting systematic educational research.

Chapter 07

Review of Related Literature

Review of related literature (RRL) refers to a systematic examination, analysis, and interpretation of previous studies, books, journals, reports, and other relevant materials related to the research problem. It helps the researcher understand what has already been studied and what remains to be explored.

7.1 Purposes of Review of Related Literature

  1. To Understand the Research ProblemClarifies concepts and helps define variables.
  2. To Identify Research GapsShows what has not been studied; avoids duplication of research.
  3. To Develop HypothesesProvides theoretical base and suggests relationships between variables.
  4. To Select Research MethodologyGuides selection of tools and design; suggests appropriate methods.
  5. To Provide Theoretical FrameworkConnects study with established theories.
  6. To Interpret FindingsHelps compare results with previous studies.

7.2 Significance of Review of Literature

  1. It strengthens the foundation of research.
  2. It ensures scientific credibility.
  3. It prevents repetition of already completed work.
  4. It improves clarity and focus of the study.
  5. It helps in operational definition of variables.
  6. It enhances researcher's knowledge and understanding.

7.3 Sources of Review of Related Literature

A. Primary Sources

Original and first-hand information. Examples: research articles, doctoral dissertations, conference papers, government reports, original documents.

B. Secondary Sources

Interpretation or analysis of primary sources. Examples: textbooks, review articles, encyclopedias, educational magazines.

C. Online Sources

Research databases, e-journals, educational websites. At Master's level, peer-reviewed journals are preferred.

7.4 Identification of Related Literature

Identification means locating relevant studies related to the research topic. The steps involved are:

  1. Define key concepts and keywords.
  2. Search library catalogues and databases.
  3. Use references from previous studies.
  4. Consult supervisors and experts.
  5. Select studies relevant to objectives and variables.

Criteria for Selection

  • Relevance to topic
  • Authenticity of source
  • Recent and updated studies
  • Methodological quality

7.5 Organization of Review of Literature

After collecting materials, the researcher must organize them systematically. The methods of organization are:

  1. Chronological OrganizationArranged according to year of publication. Shows development over time.
  2. Thematic OrganizationGrouped according to themes or variables. Most commonly used. Example groupings: studies related to academic achievement, studies related to ICT usage, studies related to mental health.
  3. Methodological OrganizationArranged based on research methods used.
  4. Geographical OrganizationBased on location (International, National, Regional studies).

Steps in Writing Review of Literature

  1. Introduction to the section.
  2. Present related studies under subheadings.
  3. Compare and analyze findings.
  4. Identify gaps.
  5. Summarize key insights.

Review of related literature is a crucial step in educational research. It provides direction, clarity, theoretical foundation, and scientific support to the study. A well-organized review ensures that research is meaningful, relevant, and original.

Chapter 08

Hypothesis: Meaning, Importance, Sources and Characteristics

8.1 Meaning of Hypothesis

Meaning

A hypothesis is a tentative statement or assumption about the relationship between two or more variables, which can be tested through research. It predicts the possible outcome of a study.

Examples
There is a significant relationship between study habits and academic achievement.
Blended learning is more effective than traditional teaching.

8.2 Importance of Hypothesis

  1. Provides Direction to ResearchIt guides the researcher in data collection and analysis.
  2. Defines Variables ClearlyIt identifies independent and dependent variables.
  3. Helps in Selecting Research DesignDifferent hypotheses require different research methods.
  4. Guides Data AnalysisHelps in selecting statistical tools for testing.
  5. Prevents Blind ResearchResearch becomes focused and purposeful.
  6. Forms Basis for ConclusionResults are accepted or rejected based on hypothesis testing.

8.3 Sources of Hypothesis

  1. Review of Related LiteraturePrevious studies suggest possible relationships.
  2. Existing TheoriesEducational and psychological theories provide foundation.
  3. Personal ObservationClassroom experiences and practical problems.
  4. Discussions with ExpertsGuidance from supervisors or specialists.
  5. Social and Educational IssuesCurrent educational trends (e.g., ICT use, inclusive education).
  6. Intuition and Logical ThinkingResearcher's insight and reasoning.

8.4 Characteristics of a Good Hypothesis

  1. Clear and PreciseFree from ambiguity.
  2. TestableMust be capable of being verified through data.
  3. SpecificClearly mentions variables and population.
  4. Based on Existing KnowledgeSupported by theory or prior research.
  5. Simple and ConciseAvoid unnecessary complexity.
  6. ObjectiveFree from personal bias or value judgment.
  7. Limited in ScopeFocused on specific variables and conditions.

8.5 Types of Hypothesis

  1. Null Hypothesis (H₀)States no significant relationship or difference. Example: There is no significant difference between the achievement of students taught through traditional and blended methods.
  2. Alternative Hypothesis (H₁)States there is a significant relationship or difference.
  3. Directional HypothesisSpecifies direction of relationship.
  4. Non-Directional HypothesisDoes not specify direction.

A hypothesis is a crucial element in educational research. It provides focus, direction, and scientific basis for investigation. A well-formulated hypothesis ensures systematic and meaningful research.

Chapter 09

Preparation of Research Proposal

A research proposal is a formal written plan of the proposed research study. It outlines what the researcher intends to study, why it is important, and how it will be conducted. It is the blueprint or roadmap of the research work and is submitted for approval before starting a dissertation or thesis.

9.1 Objectives of a Research Proposal

  1. To present the research problem clearly.
  2. To justify the need and significance of the study.
  3. To explain the research methodology.
  4. To obtain approval from supervisor or institution.
  5. To plan the research in a systematic manner.

9.2 Major Components of a Research Proposal

  1. Title of the StudyClear, concise, and specific; reflects variables and population. Example: "A Study on the Use of Educational Technology in Higher Secondary Schools."
  2. Introduction / Background of the StudyBrief explanation of the topic, context of the problem, and importance of the issue. It sets the stage for the research.
  3. Statement of the ProblemClear and specific statement that identifies variables and population. Example: "The problem of the study is to examine the effectiveness of blended learning on academic achievement of higher secondary students."
  4. Objectives of the StudyGeneral objective and specific objectives. Example: To study the level of ICT usage among students; To compare academic achievement of boys and girls.
  5. Research Questions / HypothesesResearch questions in interrogative form or hypotheses in statement form (Null/Alternative).
  6. Review of Related LiteratureSummary of previous studies, identification of research gap, and theoretical foundation.
  7. Operational Definitions of TermsDefines key terms as used in the study. Example: Academic Achievement = marks obtained in final examination; Blended Learning = combination of online and face-to-face instruction.
  8. Delimitations of the StudyScope limitations including area, population, and time period.
  9. Research MethodologyThe most important part. Includes: research method (Survey, Experimental, etc.); population and sample; tools for data collection; procedure of data collection; statistical techniques (Mean, SD, t-test, Correlation).
  10. Time ScheduleTentative plan of work (month-wise or phase-wise).
  11. Budget (if required)Estimated expenses.
  12. References / BibliographyBooks, journals, online sources in proper citation style.

9.3 Characteristics of a Good Research Proposal

  • Clear and well-structured
  • Feasible and realistic
  • Scientifically sound
  • Logically organized
  • Free from ambiguity
  • Academically formal

A research proposal is the foundation of a successful research study. Proper preparation ensures clarity, feasibility, and scientific accuracy in conducting educational research.

Chapter 10

Research Design

Research design is the overall plan, structure, and strategy of a research study conceived to obtain answers to research questions or to test hypotheses. It is the blueprint or framework that guides the researcher in collection of data, measurement of variables, analysis and interpretation, and drawing conclusions.

10.1 Need for Research Design

A research design is necessary because:

  1. It provides direction and focus.
  2. It reduces chances of bias and errors.
  3. It ensures systematic data collection.
  4. It helps in proper testing of hypothesis.
  5. It increases validity and reliability of research.
  6. It saves time, cost, and effort.

Without a research design, research becomes unorganized and unreliable.

10.2 Objectives of Research Design

  1. To answer research questions clearly.
  2. To test hypotheses accurately.
  3. To control extraneous variables.
  4. To ensure accurate measurement of variables.
  5. To plan analysis procedures.

10.3 Important Components of Research Design

  1. Statement of the ProblemClearly defines the research issue; identifies variables and population.
  2. Objectives and HypothesesSpecifies what the researcher intends to achieve; provides direction for data collection.
  3. Selection of Research MethodSurvey method, Experimental method, Descriptive method, Historical method. The choice depends on the nature of the problem.
  4. VariablesIndependent variable, Dependent variable, Control variables. Proper identification of variables is essential.
  5. Population and SampleDefines target population, specifies sampling technique, and determines sample size.
  6. Tools and TechniquesQuestionnaire, Achievement test, Interview schedule, Observation. Tools must be valid and reliable.
  7. Procedure of Data CollectionStep-by-step process and ethical considerations.
  8. Statistical TechniquesMean, Standard deviation, t-test, ANOVA, Correlation. Chosen according to hypothesis.
  9. Delimitations and LimitationsScope of study, boundaries and constraints.

10.4 Characteristics of a Good Research Design

  • Clear and logical
  • Flexible (if necessary)
  • Efficient and economical
  • Minimizes bias
  • Ensures validity and reliability
  • Suitable to research objectives

10.5 Types of Research Design

  1. Experimental DesignTrue experimental; Quasi-experimental.
  2. Non-Experimental DesignSurvey design; Descriptive design; Correlational design.
  3. Qualitative DesignCase study; Ethnographic; Phenomenological.
  4. Mixed Method DesignCombination of qualitative and quantitative approaches.

Research design is the backbone of any research study. It provides a systematic and scientific framework for conducting research. A well-prepared research design ensures that the study is reliable, valid, and capable of achieving its objectives effectively.

Chapter 11

Concept of Population and Sample; Methods of Sampling

11.1 Concept of Population

Meaning

In research, population refers to the entire group of individuals, objects, or events that the researcher wants to study. It is the total collection of elements that share certain characteristics relevant to the study.

Types of Population

  1. Target PopulationThe entire group to which the researcher wants to generalize the results. Example: All higher secondary students of Bongaigaon district.
  2. Accessible PopulationThe portion of the target population that the researcher can actually reach. Example: Higher secondary students of selected schools in Bongaigaon.

Characteristics of Population

  • Large in size
  • Defined by specific characteristics
  • May be finite or infinite
  • Forms the basis for sampling

11.2 Concept of Sample

Meaning

A sample is a small representative portion selected from the population for the purpose of study. Since studying the entire population is often impractical due to time, cost, and effort, researchers select a sample.

Characteristics of a Good Sample

  1. Representative of the population
  2. Adequate in size
  3. Free from bias
  4. Selected scientifically

Importance of Sampling

  • Saves time and cost
  • Makes research manageable
  • Allows detailed study
  • Enables statistical analysis

11.3 Methods of Sampling — A. Probability Sampling

In probability sampling, each member of the population has an equal and known chance of being selected.

  1. Simple Random SamplingEvery individual has equal chance; lottery method or random number table used. Example: Selecting 50 students randomly from a list of 500 students.
  2. Stratified Random SamplingPopulation divided into subgroups (strata); sample drawn from each group. Example: Dividing students into boys and girls and selecting randomly from each group.
  3. Systematic SamplingSelecting every nth individual. Example: Selecting every 10th student from a list.
  4. Cluster SamplingPopulation divided into clusters (e.g., schools); entire clusters selected randomly. Example: Selecting 5 schools randomly and studying all students in those schools.

11.4 Methods of Sampling — B. Non-Probability Sampling

In non-probability sampling, not every individual has an equal chance of selection.

  1. Convenience SamplingSelecting easily available subjects. Example: Surveying students present in one class.
  2. Purposive SamplingSelection based on specific purpose or criteria. Example: Selecting only science stream students for study.
  3. Quota SamplingSample selected according to fixed proportions.
  4. Snowball SamplingParticipants refer other participants; used in special cases.

11.5 Comparison of Sampling Methods

BasisProbability SamplingNon-Probability Sampling
Chance of SelectionEqual and knownNot equal
BiasLess biasMore bias
GeneralizationPossibleLimited
MethodScientificSubjective

Population refers to the entire group under study, while a sample is a representative part of that population. Proper selection of sampling method ensures reliability, validity, and accuracy in educational research.

Chapter 12

Tools of Educational Research

Tools of educational research are instruments used for collecting data systematically and objectively. Proper tools help ensure validity, reliability, and accuracy of research findings. Important tools include the Observation Schedule, Questionnaire, and Interview Schedule.

12.1 Observation Schedule

Meaning

Observation schedule is a structured tool used to observe and record behavior, events, or situations systematically according to predetermined categories. It helps collect first-hand information by directly observing subjects.

Types of Observation

  • Structured observation
  • Unstructured observation
  • Participant observation
  • Non-participant observation

Features of Observation Schedule

  • Pre-determined checklist or categories
  • Objective recording
  • Focused on specific behaviors
  • Used in natural settings

Advantages

  • Provides real-time data
  • Useful for studying classroom behavior
  • Does not depend on respondent's memory

Limitations

  • Observer bias possible
  • Time-consuming
  • Some behaviors cannot be observed directly
Example
Observing teacher-student interaction in a classroom using a prepared checklist.

12.2 Questionnaire

Meaning

A questionnaire is a written list of questions used to collect information from respondents. It is widely used in survey research.

Types of Questionnaire

  • Structured (close-ended questions)
  • Unstructured (open-ended questions)
  • Mixed questionnaire

Characteristics

  • Written format
  • Standardized questions
  • Suitable for large samples
  • Can be self-administered

Advantages

  • Economical
  • Covers large population
  • Easy to analyze statistically
  • Ensures anonymity

Limitations

  • Low response rate
  • Misinterpretation of questions
  • No opportunity for clarification
Example
Questionnaire on students' attitude towards online learning.

12.3 Interview Schedule

Meaning

An interview schedule is a structured list of questions used by the researcher during a face-to-face or telephonic interview. Unlike a questionnaire, the interviewer asks the questions and records responses.

Types of Interview

  • Structured interview
  • Semi-structured interview
  • Unstructured interview

Characteristics

  • Direct interaction
  • Flexible
  • Allows probing
  • Detailed responses

Advantages

  • High response rate
  • Clarification possible
  • Rich and in-depth data

Limitations

  • Time-consuming
  • Expensive
  • Interviewer bias possible
Example
Interviewing teachers about challenges in implementing ICT in classroom.

12.4 Comparison: Questionnaire vs. Interview Schedule

BasisQuestionnaireInterview Schedule
ModeWrittenOral
InteractionNo direct interactionDirect interaction
CostLowHigh
Response rateSometimes lowUsually high

Observation schedule, questionnaire, and interview schedule are important tools of educational research. Chapter 13

Meaning of Data and Its Types

13.1 Meaning of Data

Meaning

Data refers to the facts, figures, observations, responses, or information collected for the purpose of research. In educational research, data are collected to answer research questions or test hypotheses. Data are raw information collected from respondents for analysis and interpretation. Data may be numerical (marks, scores, percentages) or descriptive (opinions, attitudes, behaviors).

Characteristics of Data

  • Collected systematically
  • Relevant to research objectives
  • Accurate and reliable
  • Capable of analysis

13.2 Types of Data — A. Based on Nature

  1. Quantitative DataData expressed in numbers and measurable form. Examples: marks obtained by students, attendance percentage, age of students. Features: statistical analysis possible, objective and precise, used in experimental and survey research.
  2. Qualitative DataData expressed in words, descriptions, and opinions. Examples: students' opinions about teaching methods, teachers' perception of classroom environment. Features: in-depth understanding, thematic analysis, used in case studies and interviews.

13.3 Types of Data — B. Based on Source

  1. Primary DataData collected directly by the researcher for the first time. Sources: observation, interview, questionnaire, tests. Advantage: more reliable and specific to research problem.
  2. Secondary DataData already collected by someone else and used by the researcher. Sources: books, journals, government reports, previous research studies. Advantage: saves time and cost.

13.4 Types of Data — C. Based on Measurement Level

  1. Nominal DataCategorized without order. Example: Gender (Male/Female).
  2. Ordinal DataRanked or ordered. Example: 1st, 2nd, 3rd position.
  3. Interval DataEqual intervals, no true zero. Example: Temperature scale.
  4. Ratio DataEqual intervals with true zero. Example: Marks, height, weight.

13.5 Importance of Data in Educational Research

  1. Helps in testing hypothesis.
  2. Supports objective conclusions.
  3. Provides evidence-based findings.
  4. Improves educational planning and policy.

Data are the foundation of research. They provide the necessary information for analysis, interpretation, and conclusion. Proper classification of data ensures accurate and meaningful research findings in education.

Chapter 14

Collection and Organization of Data

Data collection and organization are essential steps in educational research. Accurate collection and systematic organization of data ensure valid, reliable, and meaningful research findings.

14.1 Collection of Data — Meaning and Sources

Meaning

Data collection refers to the systematic gathering of information relevant to the research problem. It is done after defining the research problem, formulating objectives/hypotheses, selecting research design, and choosing tools.

Sources of Data Collection

  1. Primary DataCollected directly by the researcher. Methods: observation, questionnaire, interview, tests and scales.
  2. Secondary DataCollected from already available sources. Sources: books, journals, government reports, previous research studies.

14.2 Steps in Data Collection

  1. Selection of appropriate tools.
  2. Pilot testing of tools.
  3. Obtaining permission from authorities.
  4. Administering tools to sample.
  5. Recording responses accurately.
  6. Ensuring ethical considerations.

Precautions in Data Collection

  • Ensure validity and reliability of tools
  • Avoid bias
  • Maintain confidentiality
  • Provide clear instructions
  • Follow uniform procedure

14.3 Organization of Data — Meaning and Steps

Meaning

Organization of data refers to arranging, classifying, and presenting collected data in a logical and systematic manner. Organization means converting raw data into meaningful form.

  1. EditingChecking errors and incomplete responses; removing irrelevant information.
  2. CodingAssigning numerical values to responses. Example: Male = 1, Female = 2.
  3. ClassificationGrouping data into categories. Types: qualitative, quantitative, chronological, geographical classification.
  4. TabulationArranging data in tables for easy understanding. Types of tables: simple table, complex table, frequency table.
  5. Graphical RepresentationBar diagram, pie chart, histogram, frequency polygon.

14.4 Importance of Organization of Data

  2. Simplifies analysis.
  3. Reduces complexity.
  4. Helps in interpretation.
  5. Facilitates statistical calculations.

Chapter 15

Analysis and Interpretation of Data

Analysis and interpretation are crucial steps in educational research. After collecting and organizing data, the researcher must analyze and interpret them to draw meaningful conclusions.

15.1 Meaning of Data Analysis

Meaning

Data analysis is the process of examining, classifying, tabulating, and statistically processing the collected data to discover patterns, relationships, or trends. Data analysis means converting raw data into meaningful information.

Objectives of Data Analysis

  1. To summarize large data into simple form.
  2. To test hypotheses.
  3. To identify relationships between variables.
  4. To compare groups.
  5. To draw valid conclusions.

15.2 Types of Data Analysis

1. Quantitative Data Analysis

Used when data are numerical.

  1. Descriptive StatisticsDescribes basic features of data. Includes: Mean, Median, Mode, Standard deviation, Percentage. Purpose: To summarize and describe data.
  2. Inferential StatisticsUsed to make generalizations about population. Includes: t-test, ANOVA, Correlation, Chi-square. Purpose: To test hypotheses; to determine significance.

2. Qualitative Data Analysis

Used when data are non-numerical (interviews, observations). Methods: thematic analysis, content analysis, coding and categorization. Purpose: to identify themes and patterns; to understand meanings and experiences.

15.3 Steps in Data Analysis

  1. Editing of data
  2. Coding
  3. Classification
  4. Tabulation
  5. Selection of statistical tools
  6. Calculation
  7. Presentation (tables, graphs)

15.4 Meaning of Interpretation of Data

Meaning

Interpretation is the process of explaining the meaning and significance of analyzed data. Interpretation means explaining what the results show. It connects findings with research objectives, hypotheses, and previous studies.

Steps in Interpretation

  1. Examine results carefully.
  2. Compare with objectives/hypothesis.
  3. Relate findings with previous research.
  4. Explain possible reasons.
  5. Draw conclusions.

Precautions in Interpretation

  • Avoid personal bias
  • Do not exaggerate findings
  • Interpret within scope of study
  • Consider limitations

Chapter 17

Major Components of the Research Report

A research report is the final written document that presents the entire research study in a systematic and scientific manner. It generally consists of three major parts: the Preliminary Section, the Main Body of the Report, and End Matter.

17.1 I. Preliminary Section

This section introduces the research work.

  1. Title PageTitle of the study, name of researcher, degree and institution, year of submission.
  2. Certificate / DeclarationApproval by supervisor; declaration of originality.
  3. AcknowledgementExpression of gratitude to those who helped.
  4. AbstractBrief summary of the entire study; includes problem, objectives, method, findings.
  5. Table of ContentsList of chapters and page numbers.
  6. List of Tables / FiguresIf any.

17.2 II. Main Body of the Report

This is the most important part of the research report. It usually consists of five chapters.

  1. Chapter I — IntroductionBackground of the study; statement of the problem; objectives of the study; hypotheses / research questions; operational definitions; delimitations of the study.
  2. Chapter II — Review of Related LiteratureTheoretical framework; review of previous studies; identification of research gap.
  3. Chapter III — MethodologyDescribes how the study was conducted. Includes: research design; population and sample; sampling technique; tools used; procedure of data collection; statistical techniques used.
  4. Chapter IV — Analysis and Interpretation of DataPresentation of data in tables and graphs; statistical analysis; interpretation of results; testing of hypotheses.
  5. Chapter V — Findings, Conclusions and SuggestionsMajor findings; conclusions; educational implications; recommendations; suggestions for further research.

17.3 III. End Matter

This section completes the report.

  1. Bibliography / ReferencesList of books, journals, websites used.
  2. AppendicesQuestionnaire, interview schedule, raw data, permission letters.
  3. IndexIf required.

The major components of a research report ensure systematic presentation of research work. Each section has a specific function, and together they provide a complete and scientific account of the study.

Chapter 19

Format / Structure of the Research Report

A research report follows a systematic format to present the study in a logical and scientific manner. It is generally divided into three major sections: the Preliminary Section, the Main Body of the Report, and the Reference Section (End Matter).

19.1 I. Preliminary Section

This section appears at the beginning of the research report and provides introductory and identification details of the study.

  1. Title PageTitle of the research study; name of the researcher; degree for which the report is submitted; name of the institution; year of submission.
  2. Certificate / Approval PageCertification by the research guide/supervisor.
  3. DeclarationStatement of originality by the researcher.
  4. AcknowledgementExpression of gratitude to individuals/institutions.
  5. AbstractBrief summary of the entire study, including problem, objectives, methodology, findings, and conclusions.
  6. Table of ContentsList of chapters and page numbers.
  7. List of Tables / FiguresIf any.

19.2 II. Main Body of the Report

This is the core part of the research report. It presents the complete research work, usually in five chapters.

  1. Chapter I — IntroductionBackground of the study; statement of the problem; objectives of the study; hypotheses/research questions; operational definitions; delimitations and limitations.
  2. Chapter II — Review of Related LiteratureTheoretical framework; review of previous studies; identification of research gap.
  3. Chapter III — Research MethodologyResearch design; population and sample; sampling techniques; tools used for data collection; procedure of data collection; statistical techniques used.
  4. Chapter IV — Analysis and Interpretation of DataTabulation and graphical presentation; statistical analysis; interpretation of results; testing of hypotheses.
  5. Chapter V — Findings, Conclusions and SuggestionsMajor findings; conclusions; educational implications; recommendations; suggestions for further research.

19.3 III. Reference Section (End Matter)

This section provides supporting and supplementary materials.

  1. References / BibliographyList of books, journals, articles, and other sources; written in a specific citation style (APA, MLA, etc.).
  2. AppendicesQuestionnaire; interview schedule; raw data; permission letters.
  3. IndexIf required.

The format or structure of a research report ensures systematic presentation of research findings. Dividing it into preliminary, main body, and reference sections helps in clarity, organization, and scientific reporting. A properly structured research report enhances readability, credibility, and academic value.
