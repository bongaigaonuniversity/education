Chapter 1

Concept and Characteristics of Teaching

Concept of Teaching

Teaching is a purposeful and systematic process in which a teacher helps learners acquire knowledge, skills, attitudes, and values. It is not just the delivery of information but an interactive process aimed at bringing desirable changes in learners' behaviour.

In simple words, teaching is the art and science of guiding learning.

According to modern educational views, teaching focuses on: facilitating learning, creating meaningful learning experiences, and helping learners think, understand, and apply knowledge.

Characteristics of Teaching

  1. Teaching is a purposeful activity. Teaching is goal-oriented. Every teaching act is done to achieve specific educational objectives.
  2. Teaching is an interactive process. It involves active interaction between the teacher and the learner. Effective teaching requires two-way communication.
  3. Teaching is learner-centred. Modern teaching focuses on the needs, interests, abilities, and pace of learners rather than only on the teacher.
  4. Teaching is both an art and a science. As an art, it requires creativity, skills, and personal style. As a science, it is based on principles, methods, and psychological laws of learning.
  5. Teaching brings behavioural change. The main aim of teaching is to bring positive and desirable changes in the learner's behaviour.
  6. Teaching is a planned process. Teaching follows planning, such as lesson plans, teaching strategies, teaching aids, and evaluation methods.
  7. Teaching is a social process. Teaching occurs in a social setting and helps learners adjust to society and culture.
  8. Teaching is continuous and dynamic. Teaching is not limited to the classroom and continues throughout life. It changes according to time, place, and learner needs.
  9. Teaching involves evaluation. Teaching includes continuous assessment to check whether objectives are achieved and to improve the teaching process.
  10. Teaching encourages learning and thinking. Good teaching promotes critical thinking, problem-solving, creativity, and understanding.
One-line ConclusionTeaching is a systematic, interactive, and purposeful process aimed at facilitating learning and bringing desirable behavioural changes in learners.
Chapter 2

Principles and Marks of Good Teaching

Principles of Good Teaching

  1. Principle of Activity. Learners learn best by doing. Teaching should actively involve students through discussions, experiments, and activities.
  2. Principle of Motivation. Teaching should arouse interest and curiosity in learners to make learning meaningful and enjoyable.
  3. Principle of Individual Differences. Teaching should consider differences in learners' abilities, interests, and learning pace.
  4. Principle of Clear Objectives. Teaching should be based on well-defined and clearly stated objectives.
  5. Principle of Psychological Readiness. Teaching should be according to the learner's mental level, age, and readiness.
  6. Principle of Correlation. Teaching should relate new knowledge to previous knowledge and real-life situations.
  7. Principle of Proceeding from Known to Unknown. Teaching should start with familiar concepts and move gradually to unfamiliar ones.
  8. Principle of Simple to Complex. Concepts should be taught in a logical sequence from simple ideas to complex ones.
  9. Principle of Learning by Doing. Practical work and experiences help in better understanding and retention.
  10. Principle of Feedback and Reinforcement. Immediate feedback helps correct mistakes and strengthens learning.

Marks (Characteristics) of Good Teaching

  1. Clear and well-defined objectives. Good teaching has clearly planned and meaningful goals.
  2. Effective communication. The teacher explains concepts clearly using appropriate language and examples.
  3. Learner participation. Students are actively involved in the teaching-learning process.
  4. Use of appropriate teaching methods and aids. Good teaching uses suitable methods, tools, and technology to enhance learning.
  5. Motivating learning environment. The classroom atmosphere is encouraging, friendly, and disciplined.
  6. Individual attention. A good teacher recognizes and supports individual learner needs.
  7. Correlation with real life. Knowledge is related to practical life situations and experiences.
  8. Continuous evaluation. Regular assessment is used to measure progress and improve teaching.
  9. Flexibility in teaching. Teaching methods are adjusted according to learner response and situation.
  10. Achievement of learning outcomes. The ultimate mark of good teaching is the successful achievement of educational objectives.
Exam-Friendly ConclusionGood teaching follows sound principles and is reflected in effective learning, active participation, and overall development of learners.
Chapter 3

Maxims of Teaching

Maxims of teaching are general guidelines or rules that help a teacher organize teaching in a logical and psychological manner so that learning becomes easy, meaningful, and effective.

Important Maxims of Teaching

  1. From Known to Unknown. Teaching should begin with what learners already know and then introduce new knowledge.
  2. From Simple to Complex. Simple ideas should be taught first, followed by complex concepts.
  3. From Concrete to Abstract. Teaching should start with real objects and experiences before moving to abstract ideas.
  4. From Particular to General. Specific examples should be given first, leading to general rules or principles.
  5. From Whole to Part. A complete concept should be presented before explaining its parts.
  6. From Easy to Difficult. Easy content should precede difficult content to build confidence and understanding.
  7. From Indefinite to Definite. Vague ideas should be clarified into definite and precise concepts.
  8. From Near to Far. Teaching should begin with the learner's immediate environment and then expand outward.
  9. From Analysis to Synthesis. Breaking down concepts into parts (analysis) and then combining them for full understanding (synthesis).
  10. From Psychological to Logical. Teaching should follow the learner's mental readiness first and then the logical sequence of the subject.
One-Line DefinitionMaxims of teaching are practical principles that guide teachers in organizing learning from easy and familiar concepts to difficult and unfamiliar ones.

Factors Affecting the Teaching–Learning Process

The teaching–learning process is influenced by several interrelated factors related to the teacher, learner, content, methods, and environment.

1. Teacher-Related Factors

  • Subject knowledge of the teacher
  • Teaching skills and methods used
  • Personality, attitude, and motivation of the teacher
  • Experience and training
  • Ability to motivate and guide learners

2. Learner-Related Factors

  • Age and maturity
  • Intelligence and ability level
  • Interest, motivation, and attitude towards learning
  • Previous knowledge and experience
  • Physical and mental health

3. Content / Curriculum-Related Factors

  • Relevance of the syllabus to learner needs
  • Organization and sequencing of content
  • Difficulty level of subject matter
  • Availability of learning materials

4. Teaching Methods and Strategies

  • Appropriateness of teaching methods
  • Use of teaching aids and technology
  • Student participation and interaction
  • Activity-based and learner-centred approaches

5. Classroom and Physical Environment

  • Classroom size and seating arrangement
  • Lighting, ventilation, and noise level
  • Availability of teaching–learning resources
  • Time duration of teaching periods

6. Psychological and Emotional Factors

  • Teacher–student relationship
  • Classroom discipline and climate
  • Learner confidence and emotional security
  • Stress, anxiety, or fear of failure

7. Evaluation and Feedback

  • Continuous assessment
  • Timely feedback and reinforcement
  • Fair and objective evaluation methods

8. Social and Cultural Factors

  • Home environment and parental support
  • Socio-economic status
  • Cultural values and language background
ConclusionThe teaching–learning process is affected by a combination of teacher, learner, curriculum, environment, and social factors. Effective teaching occurs when these factors are well balanced and supportive.

Teaching as a Science and as a Profession

Teaching as a Science

Teaching is considered a science because it is based on systematic knowledge, principles, and laws of learning. Teaching applies scientific principles drawn from psychology, sociology, and educational research to make learning effective.

Features of Teaching as a Science

  1. Based on scientific principles. Teaching follows laws of learning, motivation, and development.
  2. Systematic and planned process. Teaching involves planning objectives, methods, activities, and evaluation.
  3. Cause-and-effect relationship. Teaching methods produce measurable learning outcomes.
  4. Use of experimentation and research. Teaching strategies are tested and improved through educational research.
  5. Objective evaluation. Learning outcomes can be measured and assessed scientifically.

Teaching as a Profession

Teaching is regarded as a profession because it requires specialized knowledge, training, ethics, and social responsibility. A profession is an occupation that demands formal preparation and is guided by ethical standards. Teaching fulfills these conditions.

Characteristics of Teaching as a Profession

  1. Specialized knowledge and training. Teachers require professional qualifications and training.
  2. Defined code of ethics. Teachers follow moral and ethical principles in their work.
  3. Social service orientation. Teaching serves society by shaping responsible citizens.
  4. Professional organizations. Teachers are members of professional bodies.
  5. Continuous professional development. Teachers engage in in-service training and lifelong learning.
  6. Autonomy and responsibility. Teachers have professional freedom with accountability.
ConclusionTeaching is both a science and a profession. As a science, it is based on systematic principles, and as a profession, it requires specialized training, ethics, and commitment to social service.

Levels of Teaching

Levels of teaching refer to different stages of teaching based on the mental development of learners and the depth of learning outcomes. There are three main levels:

1. Memory Level of Teaching

Lowest Level

The memory level is the lowest level of teaching, where emphasis is on rote learning and recall of facts.

Characteristics

  • Focus on memorization
  • Learners are passive
  • Teacher-centred approach
  • Knowledge is reproduced as it is learned
  • Little scope for thinking or understanding

Methods Used

  • Lecture method
  • Drill and practice
  • Question–answer (recall type)

Learning Outcome: Recall of facts, definitions, dates, formulas.

2. Understanding Level of Teaching

Intermediate Level

The understanding level focuses on comprehension of meaning, relationships, and concepts.

Characteristics

  • Emphasis on understanding and interpretation
  • Learners are active participants
  • Interaction between teacher and students
  • Application of knowledge in new situations
  • Logical thinking is encouraged

Methods Used

  • Discussion method
  • Demonstration
  • Problem-solving
  • Questioning (why, how type)

Learning Outcome: Explanation, comparison, application, interpretation.

3. Reflective Level of Teaching

Highest Level

The reflective level is the highest level of teaching, which emphasizes critical thinking, reasoning, and problem-solving.

Characteristics

  • Learner-centred approach
  • Encourages independent and creative thinking
  • Focus on problem identification and solution
  • Emphasis on analysis, synthesis, and evaluation
  • Teacher acts as a guide or facilitator

Methods Used

  • Problem-solving method
  • Project method
  • Seminar and debate
  • Inquiry-based learning

Learning Outcome: Critical thinking, decision-making, innovation and self-directed learning.

Comparative Summary

LevelFocusThinking Level
MemoryRecallLow
UnderstandingComprehensionModerate
ReflectiveCritical ThinkingHigh
ConclusionTeaching progresses from memory level to understanding level and finally to reflective level, leading to higher-order thinking and meaningful learning.

Concept of Teaching Effectiveness and Teacher Competencies

Concept of Teaching Effectiveness

Teaching effectiveness refers to the degree to which a teacher successfully achieves the desired learning outcomes in students. It shows how well teaching helps learners understand, apply knowledge, develop skills, and grow holistically.

Key Points

  • Teaching effectiveness is outcome-oriented
  • It focuses on student learning and development
  • It depends on the quality of planning, delivery, and evaluation

Indicators of Teaching Effectiveness

  1. Achievement of instructional objectives
  2. Active learner participation
  3. Clarity of explanation and communication
  4. Use of appropriate teaching methods and aids
  5. Positive classroom climate
  6. Improvement in learner performance and behaviour

Concept of Teacher Competencies

Teacher competencies are the knowledge, skills, attitudes, and values that enable a teacher to perform teaching duties efficiently and professionally. Teacher competency reflects a teacher's ability to plan, implement, manage, and evaluate the teaching–learning process.

Types of Teacher Competencies

  1. Academic Competency: Mastery over subject matter; up-to-date knowledge of content.
  2. Professional Competency: Lesson planning skills, use of appropriate teaching methods, classroom management, assessment and evaluation skills.
  3. Pedagogical Competency: Knowledge of learning theories, understanding learner psychology, skill in using learner-centred approaches.
  4. Communication Competency: Clear verbal and non-verbal communication, questioning and feedback skills.
  5. Technological Competency: Use of ICT and educational technology, integration of digital tools in teaching.
  6. Social and Emotional Competency: Empathy and emotional intelligence, positive teacher–student relationship, ethical and value-based behaviour.
ConclusionTeaching effectiveness is the result of well-developed teacher competencies, as competent teachers are able to facilitate meaningful, learner-centred, and outcome-based education.

Phases of Teaching

Teaching is a systematic process that takes place in three main phases: Pre-active, Inter-active, and Post-active phases.

Phase 1
Pre-Active

Planning and preparation before actual classroom instruction.

Phase 2
Inter-Active

Actual execution of teaching in the classroom.

Phase 3
Post-Active

Evaluation and reflection stage after teaching.

1. Pre-Active Phase of Teaching

The pre-active phase refers to the planning stage of teaching that takes place before actual classroom instruction.

Activities Involved

  • Fixing objectives of teaching
  • Selecting and organizing subject matter
  • Choosing teaching methods and strategies
  • Selecting teaching aids and resources
  • Planning evaluation techniques
  • Preparing the lesson plan

Nature: Teacher-centred, decision-making phase, planning and preparation oriented.

2. Inter-Active Phase of Teaching

The inter-active phase is the actual execution of teaching in the classroom where interaction between teacher and students occurs.

Activities Involved

  • Presentation and explanation of content
  • Question–answer and discussion
  • Use of teaching aids
  • Motivation and reinforcement
  • Classroom management
  • Guidance and supervision

Nature: Teacher–student interaction, action-oriented phase, learner participation is maximum.

3. Post-Active Phase of Teaching

The post-active phase refers to the evaluation and reflection stage that occurs after teaching.

Activities Involved

  • Assessment of student learning
  • Testing achievement of objectives
  • Providing feedback
  • Diagnosing learning difficulties
  • Reflecting on teaching effectiveness
  • Planning remedial teaching

Nature: Evaluation-centred, reflective and corrective phase, improves future teaching.

Comparative Summary

PhaseFocusMain Activity
Pre-activePlanningDecision making
Inter-activeExecutionTeaching–learning interaction
Post-activeEvaluationFeedback and improvement
ConclusionTeaching is a continuous process consisting of pre-active, inter-active, and post-active phases, which together ensure effective planning, execution, and evaluation of learning.

Teaching Techniques (Skills)

Teaching techniques or skills are specific, observable teacher behaviours used to make the teaching–learning process effective. Important teaching skills include explaining, questioning, stimulus variation, and illustration.

1. Skill of Explaining

The skill of explaining refers to the teacher's ability to present content clearly, logically, and meaningfully so that learners can understand it easily.

Components

  • Clear and simple language
  • Logical sequence of ideas
  • Use of examples and analogies
  • Emphasis on important points
  • Checking students' understanding

Importance: Helps in concept clarification, reduces confusion, improves comprehension.

2. Skill of Questioning

The skill of questioning involves the teacher's ability to ask appropriate, relevant, and thought-provoking questions to stimulate thinking and learning.

Types of Questions

  • Recall questions
  • Understanding questions
  • Application and reasoning questions

Characteristics of Good Questions

  • Clear and precise
  • Related to lesson objectives
  • Appropriate to learners' level
  • Encourage thinking, not guessing

Importance: Maintains attention, develops thinking skills, helps in evaluation.

3. Skill of Stimulus Variation

The skill of stimulus variation refers to the teacher's ability to vary teaching behaviours to sustain students' attention and avoid monotony.

Techniques Used

  • Change in voice, pitch, and speed
  • Movement and gestures
  • Eye contact
  • Change in teaching aids
  • Change in interaction pattern

Importance: Keeps students attentive, increases interest and motivation, makes teaching lively.

4. Skill of Illustration

The skill of illustration is the teacher's ability to use suitable examples, diagrams, charts, stories, or real-life situations to explain concepts.

Characteristics of Good Illustrations

  • Relevant to the topic
  • Simple and familiar
  • Appropriate to learners' age and level
  • Interesting and meaningful

Importance: Makes abstract ideas concrete, improves understanding and retention, connects learning with real life.

ConclusionTeaching skills such as explaining, questioning, stimulus variation, and illustration play a vital role in making teaching effective, interesting, and learner-centred.

Teaching Strategies (Styles)

Teaching strategies or styles refer to the pattern of behaviour adopted by a teacher while interacting with students during the teaching–learning process. The three main teaching styles are: Autocratic, Permissive, and Democratic.

1. Autocratic Teaching Style

In the autocratic style, the teacher is the sole authority and controls all classroom activities.

Characteristics

  • Teacher-centred approach
  • Strict discipline
  • Students have little freedom
  • Teacher makes all decisions
  • One-way communication

Merits

  • Useful for maintaining discipline
  • Effective for large classes
  • Saves time

Demerits

  • Suppresses creativity
  • Learners become passive
  • May create fear or stress

2. Permissive Teaching Style

In the permissive style, students are given maximum freedom, and the teacher plays a minimal role.

Characteristics

  • Learner-centred approach
  • High student freedom
  • Minimal control by teacher
  • Informal classroom atmosphere

Merits

  • Encourages independence
  • Promotes creativity and self-expression
  • Reduces fear

Demerits

  • Lack of discipline
  • Learning objectives may not be achieved
  • Not suitable for immature learners

3. Democratic Teaching Style

In the democratic style, the teacher and students share responsibility, and learning occurs through cooperation and mutual respect.

Characteristics

  • Balanced teacher–student role
  • Two-way communication
  • Active learner participation
  • Decisions taken jointly
  • Friendly classroom environment

Merits

  • Encourages critical thinking
  • Develops leadership and responsibility
  • Enhances motivation and understanding

Demerits

  • Time-consuming
  • Requires skilled teacher

Comparative Summary

StyleTeacher RoleStudent Role
AutocraticControllerPassive
PermissiveMinimal guideHighly free
DemocraticFacilitatorActive participant
ConclusionAmong the three teaching styles, the democratic style is considered the most effective as it balances discipline with freedom and promotes active learning.

Recent Developments in the Field of Interaction Analysis

Interaction analysis — originally developed to study teacher–student interactions in classrooms — is now evolving rapidly through new methodologies, technologies, and research focuses.

1. Complex Interaction Patterns Beyond Traditional Models

Recent classroom studies are identifying more intricate interaction patterns beyond simple models like IRF (Initiation–Response–Feedback). Researchers have identified patterns such as [IⁿRⁿ]F and IⁿRⁿ that show multiple rounds of interaction between teachers and students, which encourage deeper participation and knowledge construction. Such patterns go beyond one-way talk and support more active student engagement in discourse.

2. Use of Improved Interaction Coding Systems

Classic interaction tools like Flanders or FLINT systems are still used, but researchers are applying improved versions (e.g., iFIAS) and combining them with quantitative statistical methods to analyze interaction efficacy more precisely and to understand how interaction relates to learning outcomes.

3. Technology and AI-Assisted Interaction Analysis

Artificial Intelligence (AI) and computational models are increasingly applied to analyze classroom interaction:

  • Multimodal AI models that examine nonverbal immediacy — such as gestures and facial expressions — alongside verbal behaviour to assess interaction quality.
  • Hybrid AI agents that combine expert rule-based systems with large language models to classify classroom dialogue more accurately and efficiently. This supports scalable and more detailed interaction analysis.
  • AI tools for teacher reflection: systems that automatically analyse behaviour from classroom sensors and provide objective feedback for improving teaching strategies.

These technologies help move interaction analysis from manual coding to automated, scalable analytics.

4. Interaction in Online and Blended Contexts

After the COVID-19 pandemic, research on interaction has shifted toward online and blended learning environments. Studies show that in digital environments, interaction — such as learner–instructor and learner–content interactions — plays a mediating role between student satisfaction and perceived online learning effectiveness.

5. Focus on Student-Centred and Collaborative Interaction

Current research increasingly emphasizes student-student and collaborative interaction, not just teacher talk. Methods like problem-based learning or structured group activities are analysed to see how they influence interaction patterns and learning engagement.

Summary — Exam FriendlyRecent developments in interaction analysis include: identification of complex classroom discourse patterns beyond traditional IRF; enhanced interaction coding systems and integration with statistical methods; growing use of AI and computational analysis; expanded research on interaction in online and blended learning contexts; and emphasis on student voice and collaborative interaction in classroom studies.

Taxonomy of Educational Objectives — B. S. Bloom

Bloom's Taxonomy, proposed by Benjamin S. Bloom (1956), is a classification of educational objectives that helps teachers plan, teach, and evaluate learning systematically. It classifies objectives into three domains based on learning outcomes.

I. Cognitive Domain (Knowledge Domain)

This domain deals with mental abilities and intellectual skills.

Levels of Cognitive Domain

  1. Knowledge – Recall of facts, terms, definitions (e.g., define, list, name)
  2. Comprehension (Understanding) – Grasping meaning (e.g., explain, summarize)
  3. Application – Using knowledge in new situations (e.g., apply, demonstrate)
  4. Analysis – Breaking ideas into parts (e.g., analyze, differentiate)
  5. Synthesis – Combining elements to form new ideas (e.g., create, design)
  6. Evaluation – Judging based on criteria (e.g., justify, evaluate)

II. Affective Domain (Attitude Domain)

This domain deals with feelings, emotions, attitudes, and values.

Levels of Affective Domain

  1. Receiving – Willingness to listen
  2. Responding – Active participation
  3. Valuing – Acceptance of values
  4. Organization – Integration of values
  5. Characterization – Values become part of personality

III. Psychomotor Domain (Skill Domain)

This domain deals with physical skills and motor activities.

Levels (Simpson's Classification)

  1. Perception – Awareness through senses
  2. Set – Readiness to act
  3. Guided Response – Imitation and practice
  4. Mechanism – Habitual performance
  5. Complex Overt Response – Skilled performance
  6. Adaptation – Modification of skills
  7. Origination – Creation of new movement patterns

Implications of Bloom's Taxonomy in the Teaching–Learning Process

  1. Helps in Formulating Objectives: Teachers can frame clear, measurable objectives in all three domains. Avoids overemphasis on rote learning.
  2. Guides Selection of Teaching Methods: Different levels require different methods — lecture for knowledge, discussion for understanding, projects for application and synthesis.
  3. Ensures Balanced Development: Promotes cognitive, emotional, and skill development. Supports holistic education.
  4. Helps in Evaluation and Assessment: Enables teachers to design question papers and tests covering all levels. Encourages higher-order thinking questions.
  5. Improves Teaching Effectiveness: Teaching becomes systematic and learner-centred. Lessons progress from lower to higher levels of thinking.
  6. Supports Curriculum Planning: Helps in sequencing content from simple to complex. Ensures continuity and progression in learning.
ConclusionBloom's taxonomy provides a scientific framework for organizing educational objectives and plays a crucial role in planning, teaching, and evaluating the teaching–learning process effectively.

Meaning and Components of a Model of Teaching

Meaning of Model of Teaching

A model of teaching is a systematic plan or blueprint used by a teacher to design instruction, guide classroom activities, and achieve specific educational objectives. It explains how teaching should be organized, what steps should be followed, and how teacher–student interaction should occur.

In simple words, a model of teaching provides a structured approach to teaching and learning.

Components of a Model of Teaching

Most models of teaching consist of four major components:

1. Syntax

Syntax refers to the sequence of steps or phases of the teaching model. It describes what happens first, next, and last and provides a clear procedure for classroom instruction. Example: Introduction → Presentation → Practice → Evaluation.

2. Social System

The social system defines the nature of interaction between teacher and students — the role of teacher and learners, degree of freedom or control, and type of classroom environment. Example: Teacher-centred or learner-centred interaction.

3. Principles of Reaction

These describe how the teacher responds to students during teaching — teacher's feedback, reinforcement of responses, and handling of errors. Example: Encouraging questions, giving constructive feedback.

4. Support System

The support system includes all resources and materials needed to implement the model — teaching aids, learning materials, technology and equipment. Example: Charts, textbooks, ICT tools, lab equipment.

Additional Component: Instructional and Nurturant Effects

  • Instructional effects: Direct learning outcomes intended by the model.
  • Nurturant effects: Indirect outcomes like creativity, cooperation, values.
ConclusionA model of teaching is a structured plan of instruction consisting of syntax, social system, principles of reaction, and support system, which together guide effective teaching–learning processes.

Basic Models of Teaching: ITM and CAM

Basic models of teaching are systematic instructional designs that help teachers organize learning experiences and develop specific abilities like inquiry, reasoning, and concept formation.

I. Inquiry Training Model (ITM)

Origin: Developed by Richard Suchman. Aim: To develop scientific thinking, logical reasoning, and inquiry skills among learners.

Basic Assumptions

  • Learners are curious by nature
  • Knowledge is best learned through active inquiry
  • Learning occurs through questioning and problem-solving

Phases (Syntax) of ITM

  1. Presentation of the Problem: Teacher presents a puzzling situation; students feel curiosity.
  2. Data Collection – Verification: Learners ask yes/no questions; teacher confirms or denies.
  3. Data Collection – Experimentation: Learners propose and test hypotheses; collect additional information.
  4. Formulating Explanation: Students explain the phenomenon logically.
  5. Analysis of Inquiry Process: Reflection on thinking strategies used.

Role of Teacher: Organizer and facilitator; encourages questioning; provides guidance, not answers.

Instructional Effects: Development of inquiry skills, logical and critical thinking. Nurturant Effects: Curiosity, independent learning, scientific attitude.

II. Concept Attainment Model (CAM)

Origin: Developed by Jerome S. Bruner. Aim: To help learners form and understand concepts by identifying essential attributes.

Basic Assumptions

  • Concepts can be learned by comparing examples
  • Learners actively classify information
  • Thinking improves through concept formation

Phases (Syntax) of CAM

  1. Presentation of Data and Identification of Concept: Teacher presents positive and negative examples; learners compare and analyze attributes.
  2. Testing Attainment of Concept: Learners classify new examples; verify understanding of the concept.
  3. Analysis of Thinking Strategies: Learners reflect on how the concept was formed.

Role of Teacher: Presents examples systematically; guides learners' thinking; confirms hypotheses.

Instructional Effects: Concept formation, analytical thinking, logical reasoning. Nurturant Effects: Curiosity, confidence, independent thinking.

Fixing Devices: Drill and Review

Fixing devices are instructional techniques used to reinforce, strengthen, and fix learning permanently in the minds of learners. They help in retention and recall of learned material.

1. Drill

Drill is a technique in which learners are given repeated practice of the same content to achieve accuracy, speed, and mastery.

Characteristics

  • Repetition of learning material
  • Practice-based learning
  • Teacher-directed
  • Focus on habit formation

Purpose

  • To fix facts, skills, and information
  • To develop fluency and accuracy

Examples

  • Repeated solving of math problems
  • Practice of spelling or tables
  • Language pronunciation practice

Merits

  • Improves retention
  • Develops confidence
  • Useful for skill learning

Limitations

  • May cause boredom
  • Encourages rote learning if overused

2. Review

Review is the process of revisiting learned material to reinforce understanding and correct errors.

Characteristics

  • Done after teaching
  • Helps in consolidation of learning
  • Involves discussion and questioning

Purpose

  • To refresh memory
  • To clarify doubts
  • To connect old and new knowledge

Forms of Review

  • Oral review
  • Written review
  • Question–answer session
  • Summary by teacher or students

Merits

  • Strengthens understanding
  • Helps in evaluation
  • Improves long-term retention

ConclusionDrill and review are important fixing devices that help in reinforcing learning, improving retention, and achieving mastery of knowledge and skills.
Chapter 16

Micro-Teaching: Concept, Characteristics and Objectives

Concept of Micro-Teaching

Micro-teaching is a teacher training technique in which a student-teacher practices a single teaching skill in a scaled-down teaching situation. The lesson is short, the class size is small, and the focus is on improving specific teaching skills through feedback and re-teaching.

Definition: Micro-teaching is a technique for training teachers by reducing the complexity of teaching.

Characteristics of Micro-Teaching

  1. Small class size: Usually 5–10 students.
  2. Short duration: Lesson duration is about 5–10 minutes.
  3. Focus on one teaching skill: Such as explaining, questioning, or stimulus variation.
  4. Controlled teaching situation: Teaching is done under supervision in a simulated environment.
  5. Immediate feedback: Feedback is given by supervisors, peers, or through video recording.
  6. Re-teaching and re-planning: The teacher improves the skill after feedback.
  7. Skill-based training: Emphasis is on mastery of teaching skills, not subject content.
  8. Use of observation and analysis: Teaching behaviour is observed, analyzed, and improved.

Objectives of Micro-Teaching

  1. To develop specific teaching skills: Helps student-teachers master individual teaching skills.
  2. To improve teaching behaviour: Makes teaching more effective and systematic.
  3. To provide confidence to teachers: Reduces fear and anxiety of classroom teaching.
  4. To receive constructive feedback: Enables self-evaluation and correction.
  5. To link theory with practice: Applies educational theory in real teaching situations.
  6. To prepare effective teachers: Helps in professional growth and competence.
ConclusionMicro-teaching is an effective teacher-training technique that helps in developing teaching skills through practice, feedback, and re-teaching in a controlled environment.
Chapter 17

Steps and Advantages of Micro-Teaching

Steps of Micro-Teaching

Micro-teaching follows a systematic cycle to improve specific teaching skills.

  1. Planning: Selection of one teaching skill; preparation of a short lesson plan; fixing objectives and teaching aids.
  2. Teaching: Teaching the lesson for 5–10 minutes; small group of 5–10 students; focus only on the selected skill.
  3. Feedback: Feedback given by supervisor, peers, or video recording; strengths and weaknesses are identified.
  4. Re-Planning: Lesson is modified based on feedback; weak areas are corrected.
  5. Re-Teaching: Improved lesson is taught again; same skill is practiced with corrections.
  6. Re-Feedback: Feedback is given again to assess improvement. This cycle may be repeated until mastery of the skill is achieved.

Advantages of Micro-Teaching

  1. Develops specific teaching skills: Focuses on one skill at a time for mastery.
  2. Provides immediate feedback: Helps in quick correction and improvement.
  3. Reduces complexity of teaching: Teaching is simplified by reducing class size and time.
  4. Builds confidence in teachers: Removes fear and anxiety of classroom teaching.
  5. Encourages self-evaluation: Teachers reflect on their own teaching behaviour.
  6. Links theory with practice: Helps apply educational theories in real situations.
  7. Promotes professional growth: Improves overall teaching competence.
  8. Safe and controlled environment: Mistakes can be corrected without pressure.
ConclusionMicro-teaching is an effective training technique that improves teaching skills through systematic practice, feedback, and re-teaching.
Chapter 18

Team Teaching: Concept, Steps and Advantages

Concept of Team Teaching

Team teaching is an instructional approach in which two or more teachers work together to plan, teach, and evaluate the same group of students. Each teacher contributes according to their expertise and experience, making teaching more effective and comprehensive.

In simple words, team teaching means teaching by a group of teachers as a team.

Steps of Team Teaching

  1. Planning: Teachers jointly plan the lesson; fixing objectives and content; distribution of roles among team members; selection of teaching methods and aids.
  2. Organizing: Arranging time, classroom, and resources; deciding sequence of teaching; organizing students into groups, if needed.
  3. Teaching (Implementation): Actual classroom teaching by team members; teachers may teach alternately or together; interaction among teachers and students.
  4. Observation: Team members observe each other's teaching; focus on learner response and participation.
  5. Evaluation: Assessment of student learning; evaluation of teaching effectiveness.
  6. Feedback and Improvement: Discussion among team members; identifying strengths and weaknesses; planning remedial measures.

Advantages of Team Teaching

  1. Better use of teacher expertise: Each teacher contributes according to their specialization, leading to more effective teaching.
  2. Improved quality of instruction: Joint planning and teaching result in well-organized and enriched learning experiences.
  3. Variety in teaching methods: Different teachers use different methods, reducing monotony and increasing student interest.
  4. Individual attention to students: Teachers can divide responsibilities and provide better guidance to learners.
  5. Professional growth of teachers: Teachers learn from each other through observation, discussion, and feedback.
  6. Better classroom management: Multiple teachers help in maintaining discipline and managing large classes.
  7. Encourages collaborative learning: Promotes cooperation among teachers and students.
  8. Effective evaluation: Assessment becomes more objective and comprehensive due to shared responsibility.
  9. Flexibility in teaching: Teaching plans can be easily modified based on student needs and feedback.
  10. Enhances student achievement: Students benefit from diverse perspectives and teaching styles.
ConclusionTeam teaching improves instructional quality, promotes teacher cooperation, and enhances student learning by combining the strengths of multiple teachers.
Chapter 19

Teaching Aids: Meaning, Types, Principles, Importance and Uses

Meaning of Teaching Aids

Teaching aids are tools, materials, or resources used by teachers to support, supplement, and reinforce teaching so that learning becomes easy, effective, and interesting. In simple words, teaching aids help learners see, hear, touch, and understand what is being taught.

Types of Teaching Aids

  1. Audio Aids: Appeal to the sense of hearing. Examples: Radio, tape recorder, podcasts.
  2. Visual Aids: Appeal to the sense of sight. Examples: Charts, maps, diagrams, pictures, models.
  3. Audio-Visual Aids: Appeal to both sight and hearing. Examples: Television, videos, films, smart boards.
  4. Projected Aids: Require projection devices. Examples: PowerPoint, OHP, LCD projector.
  5. Non-Projected Aids: Do not require machines. Examples: Blackboard, charts, flashcards.
  6. Activity-Based Aids: Involve learner participation. Examples: Experiments, role-play, models, games.

Principles of Using Teaching Aids

  1. Principle of purpose – Aid should serve a clear teaching objective.
  2. Principle of suitability – Appropriate to age and level of learners.
  3. Principle of simplicity – Easy to understand and use.
  4. Principle of accuracy – Correct and up-to-date information.
  5. Principle of visibility – Clearly visible to all students.
  6. Principle of integration – Integrated with the lesson, not separate.
  7. Principle of economy – Cost-effective and easily available.
  8. Principle of motivation – Should arouse interest and curiosity.

Importance of Teaching Aids

  1. Make learning interesting and enjoyable
  2. Improve attention and concentration
  3. Help in better understanding of concepts
  4. Increase retention and recall
  5. Make abstract ideas concrete
  6. Encourage active participation
  7. Support individual differences
  8. Save time and effort of the teacher

Uses of Teaching Aids

  • To introduce a lesson effectively
  • To explain difficult concepts
  • To motivate learners
  • To reinforce learning
  • To review and revise content
  • To evaluate learning outcomes
  • To promote experiential learning
ConclusionTeaching aids play a vital role in making the teaching–learning process effective by enhancing understanding, interest, and retention among learners.
Chapter 20

Lesson Plan and New Trends in Teaching–Learning

Meaning of Lesson Plan

A lesson plan is a systematic written plan prepared by a teacher to organize objectives, content, methods, teaching aids, and evaluation for teaching a particular lesson within a fixed time. In simple words, a lesson plan is a roadmap for teaching.

Importance of a Lesson Plan

  • Gives direction and confidence to the teacher
  • Ensures systematic and effective teaching
  • Helps in time management
  • Makes teaching goal-oriented
  • Facilitates evaluation of learning

Components of a Lesson Plan

  1. General Information: Class, subject, topic, date, duration.
  2. Objectives: Knowledge, understanding, application, skills, attitudes.
  3. Previous Knowledge: What students already know related to the topic.
  4. Introduction: Motivation and linking previous knowledge with new lesson.
  5. Presentation of Content: Teaching steps, methods, activities, examples.
  6. Teaching Aids: Charts, models, ICT tools, blackboard.
  7. Blackboard Work: Key points, diagrams, summaries.
  8. Evaluation: Oral or written questions to assess learning.
  9. Recapitulation: Short revision of the lesson.
  10. Home Assignment: Follow-up work for students.

New Trends in Teaching–Learning

Modern education has shifted from teacher-centred to learner-centred approaches. Some important new trends are:

  1. Learner-Centred Teaching: Focus on learner needs, interests, and abilities; teacher acts as a facilitator.
  2. Activity-Based Learning: Learning through doing and experiencing — projects, experiments, role-play.
  3. ICT-Based Teaching: Use of computers, smart boards, internet, multimedia, online resources and digital classrooms.
  4. Blended Learning: Combination of online and face-to-face teaching.
  5. Experiential Learning: Learning through real-life experiences — field visits, internships, simulations.
  6. Collaborative Learning: Learning in groups and teams — discussion, peer learning, cooperative activities.
  7. Inclusive Education: Teaching that includes diverse learners; focus on equality and accessibility.
  8. Continuous and Comprehensive Evaluation (CCE): Ongoing assessment instead of one-time exams; focus on overall development.
ConclusionA lesson plan ensures systematic and effective classroom teaching, while new trends in teaching–learning promote learner-centred, activity-based, and technology-supported education for holistic development.

Meaning and Importance of Lesson Planning

Meaning of Lesson Planning

A lesson plan is a systematic, written plan prepared by a teacher for teaching a particular topic within a fixed time period. It includes the objectives, content, methods, teaching aids, learning activities, and evaluation to make teaching effective.

In short: Lesson planning is the blueprint of teaching that guides the teacher step by step during classroom instruction.

  7. Improves Classroom Management: Class activities are pre-planned; better discipline and student participation.

Chapter 22

Criteria / Essentials of a Good Lesson Plan

A good lesson plan should have the following essential qualities:

  1. Clear Objectives: Objectives must be specific, clear, and measurable. Should cover cognitive, affective, and psychomotor domains (Bloom's taxonomy).
  2. Logical Organization of Content: Content should be arranged in a systematic and sequential order. Ideas should move from simple to complex, known to unknown, and concrete to abstract.
  3. Learner-Centered: Should consider students' age, interest, ability, and prior knowledge. Encourages active participation.
  4. Suitable Teaching Methods: Appropriate methods and strategies must be selected (discussion, demonstration, activity method, etc.). Methods should match the objectives.
  5. Use of Teaching Aids: Proper use of charts, models, blackboard, ICT tools, etc. Aids should make learning more effective and interesting.
  6. Motivation and Introduction: Lesson should begin with a proper introduction to attract students' attention. Should connect new knowledge with previous knowledge.
  7. Time Management: Proper distribution of time for introduction, presentation, practice, and evaluation. Should be completed within the allotted time.
  8. Provision for Interaction: Includes questioning, discussion, and activities. Promotes teacher–student and student–student interaction.
  9. Evaluation: Should include questions or activities to check understanding. Ensures achievement of learning objectives.
  10. Flexibility: Should be adaptable according to classroom situations. Teacher can modify it if needed.
  11. Simplicity and Clarity: Language should be simple and clear. Should not be too lengthy or complicated.
ConclusionA good lesson plan is clear, systematic, learner-centered, flexible, and evaluation-oriented. It ensures effective teaching-learning and better achievement of objectives.

Chapter 24

Herbartian Approach of Lesson Plan

Meaning

The Herbartian approach is a traditional and systematic method of lesson planning based on the ideas of Johann Friedrich Herbart. It emphasizes structured teaching, correlation of knowledge, and moral development. It is mainly used for knowledge lessons.

Five Formal Steps of Herbartian Approach

  1. Preparation (Introduction): The teacher prepares students for new learning; connects previous knowledge with the new topic; arouses curiosity and interest. Based on the maxim: "From known to unknown."
  2. Presentation: New content is presented in a clear and systematic manner; use of examples, teaching aids, explanation, and questioning; students receive new knowledge.
  3. Association (Comparison): New ideas are compared with previously learned ideas; similarities and differences are discussed; helps in better understanding. Encourages deeper thinking.
  4. Generalization: Students derive general rules, principles, or conclusions; teacher guides them to form clear concepts.
  5. Application: Students apply the learned principles in new situations; evaluation of learning takes place. Ensures learning is meaningful and permanent.

Features of Herbartian Approach

  • Teacher-centered
  • Logical and systematic
  • Emphasizes moral development
  • Follows psychological principles
  • Suitable mainly for theory subjects

Merits

  1. Well-structured and organized
  2. Ensures clarity of concepts
  3. Encourages logical thinking
  4. Useful for beginners and student teachers

Demerits

  1. Teacher-centered approach
  2. Less scope for creativity
  3. Not suitable for skill-based subjects
  4. Rigid and mechanical in nature
ConclusionThe Herbartian approach provides a systematic and organized framework for teaching, especially for knowledge lessons. However, modern education prefers more child-centered and flexible approaches.

1. Mobile Learning (M-Learning)

Mobile learning refers to learning through mobile devices such as smartphones, tablets, and laptops using internet connectivity.

Features

  • Learning anytime and anywhere
  • Use of educational apps and e-books
  • Video lectures and online quizzes
  • Instant communication

Advantages

  • Flexible and convenient
  • Promotes self-learning
  • Easy access to digital resources
  • Cost-effective

Limitations

  • Distraction due to social media
  • Requires internet access
  • Health concerns (excess screen time)

2. Blended Learning

Blended learning is a combination of traditional face-to-face teaching and online learning.

Features

  • Mix of classroom instruction and digital tools
  • Online assignments and offline discussions
  • Student-centered approach

Advantages

  • Enhances learning experience
  • Flexible pace
  • Encourages active participation
  • Better understanding through multimedia

Example: Students attend class lectures and also complete online quizzes or watch recorded videos at home.

3. Flipped Learning (Flipped Classroom)

In flipped learning, the traditional teaching process is reversed. Students study the lesson at home (through videos, materials), and classroom time is used for discussion and activities.

Features

  • Homework becomes lecture
  • Classroom becomes discussion space
  • Focus on problem-solving and interaction

Advantages

  • More active learning
  • Better teacher-student interaction
  • Encourages critical thinking
  • Students learn at their own pace

4. Google Classroom

Google Classroom is a free online platform developed by Google that helps teachers create, distribute, and manage assignments digitally.

Features

  • Paperless assignment submission
  • Announcements and feedback
  • Integration with Google Docs, Drive, Meet
  • Easy grading system

Advantages

  • Organized and time-saving
  • Improves communication
  • Accessible from anywhere
  • Promotes digital literacy
